Track 1 · Pokémon Trading Card Game

Pokémon TCG for beginners

This covers enough to sit down tonight and play a real, legal-ish game — not every ruling in the book. Read it once, keep it open during your first game, and you'll have it memorized by game three.

The goal

Each player starts with 6 Prize cards set face-down. Every time you knock out one of your opponent's Pokémon, you take one Prize card (some rare Pokémon give you 2 at once). First player to take all 6 Prizes wins.

You also win if your opponent has no Pokémon left in play, or if they're forced to draw from an empty deck on their turn.

What's in a deck

A legal deck is exactly 60 cards, built from three kinds of cards:

  • Pokémon — Basic Pokémon (played straight from your hand) and Evolutions (Stage 1, Stage 2) that you place on top of a matching Basic once it's been in play since the start of your turn.
  • Trainers — Items (play as many as you want), Supporters (only 1 per turn), and Stadiums (1 in play at a time, affects both players).
  • Energy — attach 1 per turn to a Pokémon to power its attacks. Basic Energy matches a type (Fire, Water, Grass, Lightning, Psychic, Fighting, Darkness, Metal, Fairy, Colorless).

A common beginner ratio: roughly 15-20 Pokémon, 15-20 Trainers, 10-15 Energy — but there's no fixed rule. If your attacks need 2 Water energy, run enough Water energy that you're not stuck waiting.

How a turn works

  1. Draw a card. (Not on the very first turn of the game.)
  2. Do stuff, in any order, as many times as the rules allow: play Basic Pokémon to your Bench (up to 5), evolve a Pokémon that's been in play since your last turn started, play Item cards, play 1 Supporter, attach 1 Energy card, retreat your Active Pokémon (once per turn, pay its retreat cost by discarding that many Energy from it).
  3. Attack — if your Active Pokémon has enough attached Energy to pay an attack's cost, use it. Attacking always ends your turn.
  4. Pass — you can also just end your turn without attacking if you're setting up.

Terms you'll actually hear

Active PokémonThe one Pokémon in the "front" spot that attacks and can be attacked.
BenchUp to 5 Pokémon waiting behind your Active one, safe from most attacks.
Prize cardOne of the 6 cards set aside at the start; take one each time you KO an opposing Pokémon.
WeaknessPrinted on the card — if the attacker's type matches, damage is usually doubled.
ResistancePrinted on the card — if it matches, subtract from incoming damage (usually -30).
Retreat costEnergy you discard from your Active Pokémon to swap it with a Bench Pokémon.
Knockout (KO)Damage counters equal or exceed a Pokémon's HP — it's discarded and you take a Prize.
Status conditionsAsleep, Paralyzed, Confused, Poisoned, Burned — printed effects that limit or hurt a Pokémon on future turns.
GX / EX / V / VMAXRare, more powerful Pokémon. Rule of thumb: if it says one of these on the card, it usually gives your opponent 2 Prizes instead of 1 when KO'd.
MulliganIf your opening hand has no Basic Pokémon, you show your opponent, reshuffle, and draw 7 again.

Your first game — checklist

  • Each player shuffles their 60-card deck and draws 7 cards.
  • If you have no Basic Pokémon in hand, mulligan (show your hand, reshuffle, draw again).
  • Each player sets 1 Basic Pokémon face-down as their Active Pokémon, and can place more Basics face-down on their Bench.
  • Flip all face-down Pokémon up at the same time.
  • Each player counts out 6 cards from the top of their deck, sets them aside face-down as Prizes — don't look at them.
  • Flip a coin — winner picks who goes first. The first player does NOT draw a card or attack on turn 1.
  • Play turns back and forth using the turn structure above until someone takes all 6 Prizes.

Building your first deck from what you've already pulled

You don't need to buy anything to try this. Pull out your collection and sort by Energy type first — pick the one type you have the most Basic Pokémon in (Fire, Water, whatever). That's your deck's core.

  1. Pick a type, pick your Basics. Grab every Basic Pokémon of that type, plus any Stage 1/Stage 2 evolutions that build on them.
  2. Add Colorless attackers. Pokémon with Colorless-type attacks can go in any deck — use them to round out the numbers.
  3. Add Trainers you have. Even a handful of Item and Supporter cards makes the deck feel more real; don't worry about the "perfect" build yet.
  4. Fill to 60 with Basic Energy matching your main type. If you're short on real deck size, a scaled-down 20-30 card "quick play" deck (same ratios, smaller Prize count of 3-4) is a totally fine way to learn the flow before committing to a real 60-card deck.
  5. Play a practice hand solo — just deal yourself 7 cards and see if you understand your own board before playing against someone else.

Since there are 4 of you: it's easiest to start 1v1 while everyone learns the flow, then rotate opponents. Once everyone's comfortable, a casual "everyone brings their best 20 Pokémon" battle royale (last Active Pokémon standing wins, no real Prizes) is a fun way to get all 4 playing at once.
